# Clinical Evidence Brief — Projective Play and Emotional Proxy
**Brief ID:** CEB-002
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Status:** Phase 1 Complete — Research Validated with Scope Clarification
**Bloom Garden Mechanic:** Child interacts with a companion character. Emotional expression happens through the companion as proxy — child can express feelings through what the companion "feels" without direct self-disclosure demand.

---

## Claimed Mechanism
Using a fictional companion as an emotional proxy reduces the cognitive and emotional threat of direct self-examination. Children who cannot say "I feel overwhelmed" may be able to recognize and express that state through a companion character. This emotional distance enables access to feelings that direct questioning would not reach.

---

## Supporting Evidence

### Play Therapy and Projective Mechanisms (Strong Foundation)
Ray, D.C. & McCullough, R. (2015) — Association for Play Therapy Evidence-Based Practice Statement — documents that play therapy demonstrates beneficial effects including increased self-regulation, self-concept, and social-emotional competencies. For autistic children specifically, Ware Balch and Ray (2015) found play therapy improved social-emotional competencies.

Child-Centered Play Therapy (CCPT) — 2023 doctoral study, University of North Texas, N=19 autistic children ages 5-8 — first repeated-measures design for CCPT with autistic children, found increased CCPT sessions predicted improvements in social-emotional assets and reductions in emotional and behavioral problems including Social Withdrawal and Irritability.

### Projective Play Specifically (Moderate)
Literature review of play therapy and projective techniques (Lesley University, 2023) documents that projective work in play therapy "allows small objects or toys to hold symbolic meaning" and that "projection can create meaningful and special relationships with inner emotional states and external dramatic form." The client material "tends to hold significant meaning for the client and promote engagement during the therapeutic process."

Drama therapy research (Armstrong et al., 2015 — *Drama Therapy Review*) documents that emotional expression through projection — expressing inner emotional content through dramatic representation — can produce meaningful therapeutic change. "Emotional expression is needed for change to occur in a therapeutic session."

### Play-Based Interventions in ASD (Moderate-Strong)
Scoping review — *Children* (MDPI, 2022) — 15 studies on play-based interventions in ASD found that play as a therapeutic strategy improves daily functioning in autistic children and adolescents. Play-based interventions using symbolic play showed improvements in social interaction and communication.

Systematic review (Springer Nature, 2021) — play-based interventions targeting social communication in autistic children ages 3-13 across peer-reviewed studies 2000-2020 found play-based interventions consistently support primary dimensions of ASD functioning.

PMC narrative review (2023) — Play therapy in children with autism: "It changes their way of self-expression from unwanted behaviors to more non-injurious expressive behavior using toys or activities of their choice as their words." Notes that play therapy helps children "experience feeling out various interaction styles" and aligns well with non-verbal and minimally verbal children.

DIR/Floortime (Developmental, Individual-differences, Relationship-based model) — multiple pilot studies show significant improvement in functional and emotional skills in preschool autistic children. Floortime explicitly meets the child at their developmental level and follows their lead — the same principle underlying Bloom Garden's companion interaction design.

---

## Complicating or Contradicting Evidence

**"Currently no specific play therapy is standardized for treatment"** — PMC review (2023) notes that despite promising evidence, no specific play therapy approach is standardized specifically for ASD. Evidence is promising but heterogeneous.

**Symbolic play deficits in autism:** Research consistently documents that symbolic and pretend play abilities are typically less sophisticated in autistic children than neurotypical peers. This complicates projective play as a mechanism — if the child does not engage in symbolic representation naturally, the projection mechanism may not activate equivalently.

**Mitigation for symbolic play deficit:** Bloom Garden's companion is not purely symbolic. It is a persistent, visually present character with behavioral responses. This is closer to an object play relationship than abstract symbolic projection — and object play is better preserved in most autistic children.

**Evidence quality:** Many play therapy studies with autistic children are small-sample, lack control groups, or rely on parent/teacher report. The 2023 CCPT study (N=19) is the most methodologically rigorous recent study and shows promising but not definitive results.

## Extrapolations Being Made

**EXTRAPOLATION 1 — Confidence: Moderate-High**
Projective play mechanisms are well-documented in neurotypical children and in general play therapy populations. We extrapolate that autistic children can engage in similar emotional proxy relationships with consistent companion characters, while acknowledging that the mechanism may operate differently given differences in symbolic play development.

**EXTRAPOLATION 2 — Confidence: Moderate**
Existing evidence is from in-person play therapy with trained therapists. We extrapolate that a well-designed virtual companion with consistent behavioral responses can produce similar emotional proxy dynamics in a home/independent context. The loss of the live therapist relationship is a meaningful difference that must be acknowledged.

**EXTRAPOLATION 3 — Confidence: High**
The specific design decision — companion expresses states the child can recognize and interact with rather than requiring the child to articulate their own states directly — is consistent with therapeutic principles of meeting autistic children where they are and reducing direct disclosure demands.

## Known Contraindications and Risk Factors

**Children with very limited symbolic or object play:** If a child shows no evidence of object relationship or play-based emotional expression in clinical assessment, the companion mechanic may not engage them as intended. OT should assess this at intake.

**Rigid/perseverative attachment patterns:** Some autistic children develop rigid, rule-bound relationships with characters. If a companion character behaves unexpectedly, this could cause distress rather than support regulation. Design mitigation: companion behavior is highly predictable and consistent.

**Confusing fiction with reality:** A small subset of autistic children may have difficulty distinguishing the companion's "feelings" as fictional. Parent and OT should monitor for this during pilot. No major harm vector identified but worth tracking.

## OT Validation Questions for Pilot Group

1. In your clinical practice, do autistic children in your caseload demonstrate object play — emotional relationships with toys, stuffed animals, or characters — as a pathway to emotional expression? At what developmental ages and profiles?

2. Have you used projective techniques (asking a child to describe what a stuffed animal or toy character feels) as a clinical tool? What did you observe about effectiveness across different child profiles?

3. What do you observe that indicates a child is using a character as an emotional proxy (vs. not engaging with the character emotionally at all)?

4. For children who have very limited symbolic play — what alternative mechanisms for emotional expression have you found effective?

5. Are there specific contraindications you would flag for companion-based emotional expression work that we have not addressed here?
