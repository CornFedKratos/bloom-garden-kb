# Clinical Evidence Brief — Diaphragmatic Breathing for Regulation
**Brief ID:** CEB-003
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Status:** Phase 1 Complete — Strong General Evidence, Autism-Specific Extrapolation Noted
**Bloom Garden Mechanic:** Moss the Tortoise's shell glows in a slow breathing rhythm. The child breathes with Moss — in as the shell expands, out as it softens. Bubble World uses microphone to detect breath. The breathing exercises are paced, slow, and visually guided.

---

## Claimed Mechanism
Slow, paced diaphragmatic breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system via vagal tone increase, reducing physiological arousal and supporting transition from dysregulated to regulated emotional states. App-guided visual breathing pacing (companion-led) enables children to access this regulation tool independently.

---

## Supporting Evidence

### Diaphragmatic Breathing — General Evidence (Strong)
Comprehensive systematic review — *Brain Sciences* (MDPI, 2023), 58 clinical studies including 41 RCTs, 5,407 total participants — found that slow diaphragmatic and paced breathing consistently produces significant stress and anxiety reduction across populations. Key finding: interventions incorporating slow breathing yielded significant stress/anxiety benefits in 17 of 21 clinical intervention studies.

Kramer, Neubauer & Schmiedek (2023) — *Journal of Clinical Child and Adolescent Psychology* — micro-randomized trial specifically in children's daily life found slow-paced diaphragmatic breathing exercises effective for improving subjective well-being and managing anxiety symptoms. Authors note breathing exercises are "comparatively easy to learn and to implement" making them "particularly beneficial for children."

Little et al. (2025) — *Stress and Health* (Wiley) — narrative review of breathwork literature found slow breathing (≤6 breaths per minute) consistently produces: increases in parasympathetic tone (HF-HRV, RMSSD), reductions in cortisol, reduced anxiety, and improved mood regulation. Effects observed from sessions as brief as 2-5 minutes.

MDPI systematic review (2025) — 13 studies on diaphragmatic breathing in pediatric populations ages 6-18 — found no adverse side effects reported from any RCT or clinical trial application of diaphragmatic breathing in children and adolescents. Positive effects documented include relaxation, reduced anxiety, improved academic performance, improved family relationships.

### Physiological Mechanism (Well-Established)
Slow breathing enhances top-down emotional regulation by improving amygdala and medial prefrontal cortex connectivity (Jung et al., 2024). Extended exhalation specifically activates the parasympathetic nervous system via the vagus nerve. This mechanism is well-established across multiple imaging and autonomic nervous system studies.

### App/Technology-Delivered Breathing (Emerging but Promising)
Jyskä et al. (2023) — *Healthcare* — found guided deep breathing exercises in a virtual natural environment reduced stress during pediatric treatment. The visual + guided component is important — the framework identifies human-guided or visually guided training as associated with stronger intervention effectiveness.

The 2023 systematic review framework for effective breathing interventions identifies: avoiding sessions under 5 minutes, including visual or human-guided pacing, and multiple sessions — all of which Bloom Garden's design incorporates.

---

## Complicating or Contradicting Evidence

**Autism-specific breathing evidence is thin:** The systematic reviews cited are primarily in neurotypical pediatric populations, anxious adults, and clinical populations. Specific RCTs of diaphragmatic breathing in autistic children are limited. This is the primary extrapolation risk.

**Interoceptive differences in autism:** Williams et al. (2023) — *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders* — systematic review and meta-analysis found significant interoceptive differences in autism. Autistic children may have difficulty detecting breath sensations, which could affect the effectiveness of breath-focused interventions that rely on interoceptive awareness.

**Compliance and sustained practice:** The pediatric review notes benefits in structured interventions; naturalistic daily use requires sustained engagement. App-based breathing exercises have higher dropout than clinician-delivered sessions. Bloom Garden's design (companion-led, gamified, no obligation) is specifically designed to address this, but the compliance gap remains a risk.

**Individual arousal variation:** Some autistic children are hypoaroused (under-responsive). Slow breathing for a hypoaroused child could worsen rather than help regulation. Breath-based regulation is primarily a downregulation tool and is not indicated for underaroused states.

---

## Extrapolations Being Made

**EXTRAPOLATION 1 — Confidence: High**
The physiological mechanism of diaphragmatic breathing activating parasympathetic nervous system is well-established across populations. There is no theoretical reason this mechanism would not function in autistic children.

**EXTRAPOLATION 2 — Confidence: Moderate**
Pediatric breathing effectiveness is established in neurotypical children. We extrapolate this applies to autistic children while acknowledging the interoceptive difference variable.

**EXTRAPOLATION 3 — Confidence: Moderate**
Visual/companion-guided breathing pacing will be as or more effective for autistic children than instructor-verbal guidance, given the visual processing strengths common in this population. This is theoretically supported but not directly studied in this form.

---

## Known Contraindications and Risk Factors

**Hypoaroused / underregulated children:** Slow breathing is a downregulation tool. For children presenting as underaroused or flat, this intervention is not indicated. OT should configure companion behavior away from breathing prompts for these children.

**Breath-focused anxiety:** A small subset of anxious children develop hypervigilance around breathing. Breath-focused attention could paradoxically increase anxiety for these children. This is rare but OT should screen for it.

**Interoceptive processing differences:** Children with significant interoceptive differences may not reliably feel breath sensations, reducing the effectiveness of breath-body connection work. The visual companion guide compensates for this partially — the child follows the visual, not just the sensation.

**Forced participation:** The CIL non-negotiable "no feature places emotional burden on the child" is critical here. Breathing exercises must be offered, never required. If a child is in acute distress, presenting a breathing exercise as an obligation could escalate. Design: companion models breathing, child joins if they choose.

---

## Design Implications from This Brief

The interoceptive difference finding has a direct design implication: **Bloom Garden's breathing mechanic should be primarily visual and companion-led, not primarily breath-sensation-focused.** The child follows Moss visually — the breath is secondary to the visual pacing. This is already consistent with the current design and is now evidentially supported.

The brief also supports configuring companion behavior by arousal state: OT or parent should be able to indicate if a child tends to be hypoaroused, which suppresses breathing exercise delivery in the app.

## OT Validation Questions for Pilot Group

1. In your clinical work with autistic children, how effectively do breathing-based regulation techniques work across different sensory and arousal profiles? Which child profiles respond best? Which do not respond or respond negatively?

2. Have you used visual breathing guides (animated shapes, characters breathing) with autistic children? How does this compare to verbal instruction?

3. How do you assess whether a child is hypoaroused vs. hyperaroused, and how does that affect your choice of breathing-based vs. other regulation strategies?

4. For children with interoceptive differences — what adaptations do you make to breath-based regulation work?

5. What observable indicators tell you a breathing exercise is working (regulation occurring) vs. not working or contraindicated for a specific child?
