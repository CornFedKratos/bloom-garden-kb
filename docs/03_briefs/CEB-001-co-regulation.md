# Clinical Evidence Brief — Co-regulation as Companion Mechanic
**Brief ID:** CEB-001
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Status:** Phase 1 Complete — Research Validated with Extrapolation Noted
**Bloom Garden Mechanic:** The companion is always slightly ahead of the child on the calm curve. It models regulation rather than mirrors dysregulation.

---

## Claimed Mechanism
A virtual companion that models calm regulation — rather than reflecting the child's dysregulated state — supports the child's transition to a regulated emotional state through a form of digital co-regulation. The child's nervous system synchronizes toward the companion's modeled calm.

---

## Supporting Evidence

### Co-regulation — General Foundation (Strong)
Sameroff and Fiese's (2000) transactional model establishes that a child progresses from relying on external other-regulation to developing self-regulation, with early co-regulatory relationships directly shaping long-term self-regulation capacity. This is foundational developmental psychology with strong consensus.

Buhler-Wassman & Hibel (2021) — *Infant Behavior and Development* — document that caregivers and infants co-regulate physiology, emotions, and behavior in a dynamically responsive way. Parental sensitivity is associated with improved stress-induced cortisol regulation in children.

Hendrix et al. (2022) — *Frontiers in Psychiatry* — systematic review of 20 studies on parent-mediated interventions for ASD found growing evidence that parent-mediated approaches support ER skills in young autistic children, with caregivers supporting ER development through reciprocal and transactional interactions and modeling of ER strategies.

### Co-regulation in Autism Specifically (Moderate)
Zeman et al. (2017) — *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders* — found that children with ASD often exhibit emotional problems associated with ER difficulties, and that parents play an important role in supporting ER skill development. Many parents of autistic children report that their own emotional state directly affects their child's emotional state and behavior.

Research in autism consistently shows that caregiver lack of response predicts increased likelihood of child negative emotional responses, and caregiver co-regulatory responses directly influence autistic children's regulation outcomes (Bougher-Muckian et al., 2022; Jordan et al., 2021).

Conner et al. (2021) — *Autism Research* — found emotion dysregulation is substantially elevated in autism compared to the general population, with clinically elevated ER impairment by caregiver report common across the ASD population.

### Modeling as Co-regulation Mechanism (Moderate)
Conceptualizing Emotion Regulation and Coregulation as Family-Level Phenomena (PMC, 2022) documents that caregivers' modeling of emotion regulation strategies communicates to their child what is normative and expected. When caregivers model calm regulation, children's nervous systems are scaffolded toward that state.

---

## Complicating or Contradicting Evidence

**The social referencing gap:** Co-regulation research is predominantly conducted in caregiver-child dyads involving real humans. Whether a virtual companion produces equivalent neurobiological co-regulation effects (cortisol synchrony, autonomic nervous system entrainment) is not established in the peer-reviewed literature as of early 2026.

**Attachment specificity:** Some research suggests co-regulation effects are strongest in the context of established attachment relationships. Whether a child can form sufficient quasi-attachment to a virtual companion to trigger genuine co-regulatory responses is an open empirical question.

**Individual variation:** Co-regulation responsiveness varies significantly across autistic children. Children with high alexithymia (difficulty identifying their own emotional states) may not benefit equivalently from modeling-based approaches.

---

## Extrapolations Being Made

**EXTRAPOLATION 1 — Confidence: Moderate**
The mechanism of modeling-toward-calm as co-regulation is well-supported in human caregiver-child dyads. We are extrapolating that a sufficiently engaging virtual companion can produce analogous nervous system scaffolding effects. This is theoretically plausible but not directly studied in this specific form.

**EXTRAPOLATION 2 — Confidence: Low-Moderate**
Research on children's parasocial relationships with media characters (e.g., Mister Rogers' Neighborhood research by Schmitt et al.) suggests children can form meaningful emotional bonds with consistent media characters. However, direct evidence for autistic children's co-regulatory responses to virtual companions is limited.

**Mitigation:** The companion mechanic is designed as a regulatory support layer, not a therapeutic claim in isolation. The OT prescribes exercises; the companion models calm during them. The co-regulation is additive, not the sole mechanism of effect.

---

## Known Contraindications and Risk Factors

**Distress amplification risk (MITIGATED by design):** Original proposal had companion mirroring child dysregulation. This was explicitly rejected in product design due to evidence that seeing a distressed other can escalate rather than regulate dysregulation, particularly in children with strong empathic responses. Current design: companion leads toward calm, never mirrors distress. This mitigates the primary contraindication.

**Emotional responsibility burden:** For anxious children, a companion that "needs" the child could increase cognitive load during dysregulation. Current design: companion does not need the child. It invites the child. This mitigates the primary risk.

**Attachment disruption on data loss:** For autistic children with strong attachment patterns, loss of companion data could be distressing. Architecture decision: companion identity is sacred data requiring full backup and recovery.

**Highly alexithymic children:** May not register or respond to social/emotional cues from a virtual companion. Parent and OT should monitor engagement. No harm vector identified — simply may be less effective for this subpopulation.

## OT Validation Questions for Pilot Group

1. In your clinical experience, do autistic children in your caseload form meaningful emotional relationships with consistent fictional characters or virtual beings? At what approximate ages and profiles does this occur most reliably?

2. Have you observed co-regulation effects when children watch or interact with calm characters or figures (TV, books, toys)? What behaviors indicate co-regulation is occurring versus not?

3. For children with high alexithymia in your caseload — how do you assess whether modeling-based approaches are appropriate?

4. What observable behaviors in a session would indicate a child is co-regulating with the virtual companion versus simply watching it?

5. Are there specific autistic profiles (sensory profiles, communication profiles, anxiety profiles) for which you would not recommend a companion-based approach?
