# Clinical Evidence Brief — Proprioceptive Input for Sensory Regulation
**Brief ID:** CEB-004
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Status:** Phase 1 Complete — Strong Evidence Base, App-Delivery Extrapolation Noted
**Bloom Garden Mechanic:** Stone Stacker, Tide Keeper, and Slow Motion World engage proprioceptive awareness through touch pressure, device tilt, and deliberate slow movement interactions. Cedar the Beaver's heavy-work activities mirror proprioceptive input principles.

---

## Claimed Mechanism
Proprioceptive input — the sensory system that processes body position, movement, and pressure — helps modulate arousal levels in autistic children. Activities that provide proprioceptive input support self-regulation by engaging the nervous system's natural downregulation pathways. App mechanics that require deliberate pressure, slow movement, and position awareness simulate proprioceptive engagement within the constraints of a touchscreen device.

---

## Supporting Evidence

### Sensory Integration and Proprioception in ASD (Strong Foundation)
MDPI Systematic Review (2024) — 16 studies, 2013-2023, PRISMA methodology — concludes that "intervention with sensory integration in infants with ASD meets the criteria to be considered an evidence-based practice." Sensory integration theory, originally developed by A. Jean Ayres, focuses primarily on vestibular, proprioceptive, and tactile sensory systems.

Roberge & Crasta (2022) — *American Journal of Occupational Therapy* — systematic review of sensory interventions for autistic children found Sensory Integration Therapy had Moderate evidence level for effectiveness in addressing attention and self-regulation. Conclusion: "sensory interventions appear to be effective when addressing attention and self-regulation for children with autism."

SenITA RCT (Randell et al., 2022) — *Health Technology Assessment*, 26 weeks, manualized sensory integration therapy vs. usual care in autistic children — provides the most rigorous recent RCT evidence for sensory integration therapy effectiveness.

Frontiers in Psychology (2023) — study on sensory integration training with fNIRS measurement — found sensory integration training improves balance function and executive function in autistic children, with enhanced neural activation in prefrontal cortex regions, suggesting both sensory-motor and cognitive benefits.

### Proprioception and Arousal Modulation Specifically (Moderate)
ScienceDirect RCT (2025) — vestibular and proprioceptive exercises in autistic children, single-blind RCT, 8 weeks, November 2022-November 2023 — found statistically significant effects. Notes: "According to Sensory Integration Theory, vestibular and proprioceptive inputs help modulate arousal levels, enhancing self-regulation and reducing excessive motor activity." Distinguishes ASD hyperactivity as "predominantly sensory-driven, linked to difficulties in sensory processing and self-regulation" — suggesting sensory-targeted interventions are particularly appropriate.

Case-Smith & Arbesman (2008) — *American Journal of Occupational Therapy* — landmark evidence-based review establishing sensory integration and sensory-based interventions as the most directly OT-relevant category for ASD, with proprioceptive, vestibular, and tactile systems as the primary targets.

### Heavy Work as Proprioceptive Regulation Tool (Moderate)
Heavy work activities — pushing, pulling, carrying, resistance-based movement — are widely used by OTs for arousal regulation in autistic children. The evidence base for heavy work specifically is moderate (primarily clinical consensus and case-level evidence) rather than RCT-level, but is deeply embedded in OT practice. Cedar the Beaver's building activities are modeled on this principle.

## Complicating or Contradicting Evidence

**Evidence quality debates:** The sensory integration field has ongoing debates about evidence quality. Some systematic reviews rate the overall evidence as moderate rather than strong, noting heterogeneity in study designs, outcome measures, and fidelity to SI protocols. The AOTA practice guidelines support SI but acknowledge evidence gaps.

**App-delivered proprioception limits:** True proprioceptive input requires real physical movement against resistance — the body moving through space, handling weighted objects, pushing or pulling. A touchscreen app can engage fine motor proprioception (finger pressure, precision touch) and vestibular-adjacent input (device tilt) but cannot replicate full-body proprioceptive input. This is the most significant extrapolation in this brief.

**Sensory profile variability:** Approximately 90% of autistic children have some form of atypical sensory processing, but the direction of that atypicality varies dramatically. Sensory-seeking children may respond very differently to low-intensity proprioceptive input than sensory-avoiding children. A one-size approach does not fit this population.

## Extrapolations Being Made

**EXTRAPOLATION 1 — Confidence: High**
The theoretical and evidential basis for proprioceptive input supporting arousal regulation in autistic children is strong. Sensory integration therapy meets criteria as evidence-based practice.

**EXTRAPOLATION 2 — Confidence: Low-Moderate — MOST IMPORTANT FLAG**
App-based mechanics that simulate proprioceptive engagement (touch pressure, device tilt, slow deliberate movement) are not equivalent to full-body proprioceptive input used in clinical SI therapy. We are claiming a lighter version of the mechanism — **proprioceptive awareness** rather than **therapeutic proprioceptive input**. This distinction must be clearly maintained in all product communications.

This means: Bloom Garden's touch-based mechanics are designed to support proprioceptive **awareness** and to prompt children toward regulation-supporting behaviors — they do not replicate the clinical intensity of SI therapy. The OT prescribes the real SI work; Bloom Garden extends awareness and habit between sessions.

**EXTRAPOLATION 3 — Confidence: Moderate**
Slow, deliberate, controlled touchscreen interaction (Stone Stacker, Tide Keeper) engages top-down motor regulation processes that are related to — but not identical to — proprioceptive regulation. The deliberate movement requirement may support regulation through motor planning and impulse control pathways as much as proprioceptive pathways.

## Known Contraindications and Risk Factors

**Sensory-avoiding profiles:** Children with tactile or proprioceptive defensiveness may find touch-heavy interactions aversive. The OT portal must allow configuration to reduce or eliminate touch-pressure mechanics for these children.

**Tactile hypersensitivity:** Some autistic children have extreme touchscreen sensitivity. Device settings for touch sensitivity and the app's touch requirement design must account for this.

**Sensory seeking escalation:** For sensory-seeking children, low-intensity proprioceptive app input may not be sufficient to satisfy sensory needs and could increase seeking behavior rather than regulate it. OT should assess whether app mechanics are calibrated to each child's sensory profile.

**Scope boundary:** If an OT or parent interprets app mechanics as a substitute for SI therapy, this creates clinical risk. Product messaging must be explicit: Bloom Garden supports proprioceptive awareness and extends OT goals between sessions. It does not provide SI therapy.

---

## Design Implications from This Brief

**The scope language matters enormously.** In all product communications, OT portal documentation, and parent guides, Bloom Garden's proprioceptive mechanics must be described as:
- Supporting proprioceptive awareness
- Extending OT-prescribed goals between sessions
- Encouraging regulation-supporting behaviors

Never as:
- Providing sensory integration therapy
- Replacing proprioceptive OT intervention
- Delivering therapeutic proprioceptive input

**OT configuration is essential for this mechanic more than any other.** The sensory profile variability in this population means that what regulates one child may dysregulate another. Touch-intensity mechanics and movement-based mechanics should be OT-configurable from the portal.

## OT Validation Questions for Pilot Group

1. How do you assess a child's sensory profile for proprioceptive needs — seeking vs. avoiding vs. mixed? What assessment tools do you use?

2. In your clinical experience, do children with sensory-seeking profiles respond differently to low-intensity proprioceptive input than to high-intensity? What is the typical response to mild touch-based activities for a sensory seeker?

3. How do you use heavy work in home programs? What guidance do you give parents for implementing proprioceptive activities between sessions?

4. For children with tactile defensiveness — are there any touch-based app interactions you would consider appropriate, or would you recommend avoiding touch mechanics entirely for this profile?

5. What would observable regulation improvement look like during a touch-based app interaction that would indicate the proprioceptive awareness mechanism is working?

6. How important is it to you, as a clinician, that product communications clearly distinguish "proprioceptive awareness support" from "sensory integration therapy"? What language would you recommend?
