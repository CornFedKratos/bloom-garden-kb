## Executive Summary

No direct competitor exists for what Bloom Garden is building. The market is fragmented across four categories — none of which address the full connected care system (child + parent + OT) that Bloom Garden defines. The white space is real, validated, and significant.

The "Bloom Garden" name as an app title is currently in use by an unrelated iOS casual puzzle game (PIXON PTE. LTD., flower-sorting game, App Store ID 6748257880). **Trademark and naming review is required before launch.** See name recommendation section at the end.

## Market Category Map

### Category 1 — Pediatric Emotional Regulation Apps
Direct competitors in spirit, not in execution.

---

### Breathe, Think, Do with Sesame
**Developer:** Sesame Workshop (nonprofit)
**Platform:** iOS, Android
**Price:** Free
**Target Age:** 2–5
**Clinical Backing:** Research-based curriculum, no direct RCT on the app itself (per One Mind PsyberGuide)

**What They Do Well**
The gold standard for preschool emotional regulation apps. The proxy mechanic — help the monster breathe, the monster feels better — is the closest existing analogue to Bloom Garden's companion mechanic. Completely free, no ads, bilingual (English/Spanish). Parent section is genuinely useful with tips, customizable affirmations, and breathing exercises. Beloved by OTs and parents. Five scenarios with relatable everyday challenges. Intuitive for very young children.

**What They Do Poorly**
Only five scenarios — extremely limited content, no expansion since original release. Stability issues reported across both platforms (crashing). No parent dashboard or data. No OT integration whatsoever. Age ceiling of 5 means it doesn't grow with the child. No personalization beyond recording affirmation phrases. No companion relationship — the monster is a prop, not a relationship. No ongoing engagement mechanic — children complete it and move on.

**Autism-Specific Suitability**
Moderate. The proxy mechanic works well. However the app requires reading comprehension for some elements, uses literacy-dependent instructions, and has no sensory safety design. Not designed for the autism population specifically — incidentally useful.

**What Bloom Garden Learns**
The proxy/companion mechanic validation is extremely valuable — this is the most evidence-adjacent analog to our companion design. The parent affirmation customization is a good feature to consider. The "help the character" framing over "help yourself" framing is confirmed as effective for this age range.

**Pitfalls to Avoid**
Stagnant content is a retention killer. Five scenarios is not enough. Stability and technical quality at launch is non-negotiable. The app must not feel like it has a ceiling.

### Daniel Tiger's Grr-ific Feelings
**Developer:** Fred Rogers Productions
**Platform:** iOS, Android
**Price:** Free (PBS Kids)
**Target Age:** 2–6
**Clinical Backing:** Based on SEL curriculum, strong PBS Kids research backing

**What They Do Well**
Exceptional emotional vocabulary building. "Grr-ific" framing normalizes big feelings. Daniel Tiger is one of the most trusted character relationships in early childhood. Strong parent resources. Free with no ads. Consistent with the broader Daniel Tiger ecosystem (TV show, books). Songs function as emotional regulation anchors — children carry them into real life.

**What They Do Poorly**
No OT integration. No parent dashboard. No data. Very limited content. Designed for neurotypical emotional vocabulary development, not for dysregulation in the moment. The song-based approach works for neurotypical children — for autistic children with different auditory processing, it may be overwhelming or ineffective. No personalization.

**Autism-Specific Suitability**
Low-Moderate. Useful for emotional vocabulary. Not designed for acute dysregulation support. Neurotypical design assumptions throughout.

**What Bloom Garden Learns**
Songs as portable regulation tools — children internalize them and use them outside the app. This is the mechanism Bloom Garden's Mood Orchestra touches. The consistency of a beloved character across media reinforces attachment.

**Pitfalls to Avoid**
Do not assume what works in typical SEL translates directly to autistic children's regulation needs.

---

### Moshi Kids: Sleep, Relax, Play
**Developer:** Moshi Kids Ltd (BAFTA award-winning)
**Platform:** iOS, Android
**Price:** Free trial, $7.99/month or $39.99/year
**Target Age:** 0–12
**Clinical Backing:** NYU study (n=30, 10 days) showing children fall asleep 28 minutes faster, sleep 22 minutes longer, 50% fewer night wakings

**What They Do Well**
The best sound design in the children's wellness app category. Completely ad-free. Consistent characters build genuine child attachment over time (SleepyPaws the Koala, Nodkins the Bunny). Clinically studied sleep outcomes. Excellent for wind-down and bedtime regulation. Broad age range. Offline capable. The storytelling quality is exceptional — BAFTA-winning creative team shows.

**What They Do Poorly**
Primarily a sleep/audio app — limited daytime regulation utility. No OT integration. No parent insight beyond basic usage. No emotional data capture. Subscription pricing creates access barriers for lower-income families. Overdependence risk documented (children unable to sleep without app). No companion relationship — characters are storytellers, not co-regulators. Privacy concerns noted by Mozilla Foundation (data sharing for advertising).

**Autism-Specific Suitability**
Moderate for sleep support. Not designed for autism population. Auditory-heavy design without visual sensory options may not suit all sensory profiles. No sensory design considerations.

**What Bloom Garden Learns**
Sound design investment pays off enormously — Moshi's retention is driven by audio quality. The bedtime ritual attachment (children requesting Moshi at bedtime) is exactly the "brushing teeth" habit loop Bloom Garden targets for regulation routines. Character continuity and naming drives emotional attachment.

**Pitfalls to Avoid**
Overdependence risk is real — Bloom Garden's no-obligation design philosophy is the mitigation. Privacy practices must be ironclad from day one — Mozilla-level scrutiny will come.

### Finch: Self-Care Pet
**Developer:** Finch Care Inc.
**Platform:** iOS, Android
**Price:** Free / Finch Plus ~$70/year
**Target Age:** 12+ (recommended)
**Clinical Backing:** Light — gamification of habit formation, no autism-specific research

*(Analyzed in detail in founding session log — summarized here for completeness)*

**What They Do Well**
Strong gamification loop, genuine emotional resonance, large loyal user base, mood trending, breathing exercises, daily check-ins, generous free tier.

**What They Do Poorly**
Obligation mechanics (streaks, pet neglect), daily task pressure, reading-dependent, no OT integration, no parent layer, not designed for autistic children, social features add complexity. Finch's core loop is wrong for the autism pediatric population.

**Bloom Garden's Differentiation**
Bloom Garden is not competing with Finch. We serve a population Finch was never designed for, with a clinical foundation Finch doesn't have, and a connected care system Finch doesn't offer. The comparison is instructive for what not to build, not for what to replicate.

## Category 2 — Autism-Specific Support Tools

---

### Choiceworks
**Developer:** Bee Visual LLC
**Platform:** iOS only
**Price:** $29.99 one-time
**Target Age:** 3+
**Clinical Backing:** Widely OT-recommended, no formal RCT

**What They Do Well**
The most OT-recommended visual schedule app in the category. Deeply customizable — parents photograph real objects, record their own voice, build boards specific to their child. Four board types: Schedule, Waiting, Feelings, Feelings Scale. No subscription, one-time purchase. Used in classrooms and homes worldwide for over a decade. Directly addresses transition anxiety and daily routine support. Genuinely valued by OTs as a clinical tool.

**What They Do Poorly**
Purely functional — no joy, no engagement for the child beyond task completion. iOS only (excludes Android families). Battery drain issue unresolved and developer dismissive of user concerns. No emotional data capture. No OT portal — OTs recommend it but can't interact with it. No companion or character relationship. Requires significant adult setup time to be useful. The app does the work of the schedule, not the work of regulation.

**Autism-Specific Suitability**
High for visual scheduling and transition support. Limited for emotional regulation in the moment. Not designed for dysregulation intervention — designed for routine predictability.

**What Bloom Garden Learns**
OTs actively recommend Choiceworks — this means OTs are willing to incorporate apps into their clinical recommendations. The customization model (parent adds real photos, real audio) creates deep personal relevance. One-time purchase removes subscription friction for the most price-sensitive families. The gap Choiceworks leaves — emotional regulation in the moment, OT interaction, clinical data — is exactly where Bloom Garden operates.

**Pitfalls to Avoid**
Developer dismissiveness of legitimate user concerns is a trust-killer in this community. Battery drain and technical issues disproportionately affect this population (devices are lifelines). Android exclusion is a market limitation Bloom Garden must not replicate.

---

### Zones of Regulation (curriculum/app)
**Developer:** Social Thinking
**Platform:** iOS, web
**Price:** App ~$9.99; curriculum materials additional cost
**Target Age:** 4–Adult
**Clinical Backing:** Widely used in schools and OT practice, curriculum-based evidence, no large-scale RCT specifically on the app

**What They Do Well**
The most clinically embedded regulation framework in the autism/OT space. OTs know it, schools use it, parents have heard of it. The four-zone framework (Blue/Green/Yellow/Red) gives children a shared vocabulary for emotional states. Therapist-designed. Strong professional credibility. The curriculum extends into physical materials, which increases adoption in clinical settings.

**What They Do Poorly**
Heavily literacy-dependent — the app assumes reading ability, significantly limiting utility for non-verbal and early-reader autistic children. Abstract concept presentation without visual/sensory grounding. The app itself is relatively basic — the curriculum is the product, not the app. Expensive when full curriculum is factored in. No OT portal, no data, no companion. Designed for teaching in structured settings, not for in-the-moment regulation support.

**Autism-Specific Suitability**
High for verbal, school-age autistic children with sufficient literacy. Low for non-verbal, pre-literate, or very young autistic children — a large portion of the target population.

**What Bloom Garden Learns**
The zone framework's clinical credibility is significant — OTs trust it. Bloom Garden should not replicate it (it's copyrighted and belongs to Social Thinking) but should be aware that OTs may use both tools in complementary ways. The vocabulary framework is worth referencing in exercise library entries.

**Pitfalls to Avoid**
Literacy dependency excludes a major portion of the autism population. Abstract emotional concepts without sensory grounding don't work for many autistic children. Bloom Garden's non-verbal-first design is validated by Zones' failure mode.

### Smiling Mind
**Developer:** Smiling Mind (Australian nonprofit)
**Platform:** iOS, Android, Web
**Price:** Free
**Target Age:** 7+ (youth programs)
**Clinical Backing:** Partnerships with Australian government and research institutions, clinical studies on mindfulness outcomes

**What They Do Well**
Completely free, no ads, nonprofit mission-driven. Strong clinical partnerships — validated mindfulness programs. Age-segmented content (children, teens, adults, workplace). One of the most credible clinical partnership models in the wellness app space. Australian government partnerships lend institutional credibility. Evidence base is more robust than most competitors.

**What They Do Poorly**
Not designed for autism population. No OT integration. No parent-child connected layer. Content is mindfulness-focused — limited sensory regulation utility. Adult-feeling interface despite youth content. No companion relationship. Limited engagement mechanics. Not designed for non-verbal users.

**Autism-Specific Suitability**
Low. Mindfulness approaches that rely on language, self-reflection, and interoceptive awareness are challenging for much of the autism population.

**What Bloom Garden Learns**
The clinical partnership model — government and research institution backing — is the credibility pathway Bloom Garden should pursue post-launch. The nonprofit mission alignment and free access model creates trust with healthcare professionals. Bloom Garden's OT partnership model is the analogue of this.

**Pitfalls to Avoid**
Mindfulness content that requires interoceptive awareness or language-based reflection will not serve much of the autism population well.

## Category 3 — OT Clinical Support Tools

---

### TheraPlatform
**Developer:** TheraPlatform Inc.
**Platform:** Web, iOS, Android
**Price:** From $39/month (therapist)
**Target Age:** All (clinical tool)
**Clinical Backing:** HIPAA-compliant telehealth platform

**What They Do Well**
Full telehealth + documentation platform. HIPAA compliant. OT workflow integration. Progress note templates. Insurance billing. Video sessions. Solid professional-grade clinical infrastructure.

**What They Do Poorly**
Zero child engagement layer — this is a clinician tool, not a child-facing product. No home program gamification. No parent emotional insight layer. Expensive for independent OTs. Requires OT adoption, not parent or child adoption. Not designed for between-session support.

**Autism-Specific Suitability**
N/A — clinical infrastructure, not therapeutic content.

**What Bloom Garden Learns**
OTs already pay for clinical software. The OT Practice tier pricing ($99/month) is within the range OTs pay for clinical tools — this validates the B2B pricing model. HIPAA infrastructure is already expected and accepted by OTs.

---

### Brighthearts
**Developer:** Brighthearts
**Platform:** iOS, Android
**Target Age:** Pediatric patients
**Clinical Backing:** Developed with clinicians for procedural anxiety in children

**What They Do Well**
Strong mechanic for acute anxiety reduction in clinical settings. VR-capable distraction therapy for medical procedures. Clinician-designed. Good sensory design for distraction. Proof of concept that apps can have meaningful clinical outcomes in pediatric settings.

**What They Do Poorly**
Designed for acute clinical settings (medical procedures) — not for home daily regulation. Very narrow use case. Requires clinical administration.

**What Bloom Garden Learns**
A well-designed child-facing clinical app can achieve genuine clinical outcomes and institutional adoption. The sensory distraction mechanic for acute anxiety is relevant to Bloom Garden's post-meltdown calm mode design.

### Grace App
**Developer:** IDC Design Ltd
**Platform:** iOS, Android
**Target Age:** Non-verbal/minimally verbal autism
**Price:** $37.99

**What They Do Well**

**What They Do Poorly**

**What Bloom Garden Learns**

## The White Space — What Nobody Has Built

After analyzing 12 tools across 4 categories, the gap is clear and consistent across every competitor:

| Feature | Any Competitor? |
|---|---|
| Child-facing regulation tool designed for autism specifically | Partial (Breathe Think Do, Choiceworks) |
| Companion-based co-regulation mechanic | No |
| No failure states, no obligation, curiosity-based engagement | No |
| HIPAA-compliant child emotional data capture | No |
| OT portal with exercise prescription | No |
| Parent insight dashboard with behavioral pattern data | No |
| Clinically validated exercise library with outcome tracking | No |
| AI-assisted pattern detection for OT session prep | No |
| Connected care system: child + parent + OT in one platform | No |
| Sensory-safe design as first principle | No |

Bloom Garden doesn't have a direct competitor. The closest is Breathe Think Do with Sesame for the child-facing mechanic, and Choiceworks for OT recommendation credibility. Neither has the connected care system, the clinical data layer, or the OT portal.

## Competitive Risks to Monitor

**1. Sesame Workshop updating Breathe Think Do**
The app has been largely dormant since 2013. If Sesame Workshop invests in a major update, they have brand credibility, clinical relationships, and free access that would be difficult to compete with directly. Bloom Garden's moat: OT portal, data layer, HIPAA compliance, autism-specific design, and the exercise library.

**2. Large wellness app entering pediatric autism space**
Calm, Headspace, or a similar player could develop an autism-specific product. Their moat: brand, distribution, content budget. Bloom Garden's moat: clinical depth, OT integration, HIPAA compliance, and being built by someone who lives this.

**3. Choiceworks adding engagement layer**
If Bee Visual adds a character/companion layer to Choiceworks, they already have OT adoption. Bloom Garden's moat: the companion co-regulation mechanic is architecturally central to our product, not an add-on. Also, Choiceworks is iOS only — Bloom Garden is cross-platform from day one.

**4. Academic research group building a validated tool**
Clinical credibility risk. Bloom Garden's mitigation: build the OT validation and clinical partnership layer before any academic competitor does. The pilot OT group and the CIL framework are the defense.

## Name Recommendation

Given that "Bloom Garden" is occupied in the App Store by a casual game (PIXON PTE. LTD.) and multiple clinical practices use "Bloom" in their branding in the autism space, a name change is strongly recommended before trademark filing and App Store submission.

**The naming risk:**
- App Store search for "Bloom Garden" returns the casual puzzle game first
- "Bloom" psychology practices in the autism space create brand confusion
- Trademark registration of "Bloom Garden" in the health/wellness app category will face challenges

**Recommended territory:** Names that are distinctive, ownable, warm, and hint at the companion relationship without describing the garden directly. The companion relationship is the emotional heart of the product — the name should gesture toward that.

**Shortlist for consideration:**
- *Grove* — short, ownable, implies a living world, no existing App Store conflicts in this category
- *Tend* — verb as name, implies gentle care without pressure, distinctive
- *Steady* — captures what the companion does, emotionally resonant, no direct competitor
- *Beside* — captures co-regulation perfectly, relationship-forward, highly distinctive
- *Verdant* — ownable invented-feel word meaning lush and growing, strong trademark potential

**Recommendation:** Do not lock the name until OT pilot conversations begin. Test in clinical context: "Have you tried [name] with your patients?" The name that lands naturally in that sentence wins.
